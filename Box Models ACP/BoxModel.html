Eclipses: When the Sky Changes

Types of Eclipses

  • Lunar Eclipse
  • Solar Eclipse

A lunar eclipse happens when Earth moves between the Sun and the Moon, causing Earth’s shadow to fall across the Moon. During some lunar eclipses, the Moon can appear reddish, which is why it is sometimes called a “Blood Moon.” This color occurs because Earth’s atmosphere bends red and orange light toward the Moon. Lunar eclipses only happen during a full moon and are safe to observe without special equipment. They can be total, partial, or penumbral depending on how much of the Moon enters Earth’s shadow.


A solar eclipse occurs when the Moon passes between the Sun and Earth, blocking part or all of the Sun’s light. This creates a shadow on Earth and makes the Sun appear partially or fully covered for a short time. Solar eclipses happen during a new moon and are only visible from certain locations on Earth. Because looking directly at the Sun can damage your eyes, special eclipse glasses or safe viewing methods are required. Types of solar eclipses include total, partial, and annular.
