The Origins and Impacts of Natural Disasters

  • Volcanos
  • Earthquakes
  • Droughts
  • Tornadoes
  • Sandstorms

Volcanoes: Driven by the buildup of pressure from molten magma and gases escaping through cracks in the Earth's crust.
Earthquakes: Caused by the sudden release of energy when tectonic plates shift, slip, or collide along fault lines.
Drought: Resulting from extended periods of low rainfall and high temperatures that dry out the soil and water supplies.
Sandstorms: Triggered by strong winds blowing across dry, loose sand or soil in arid and desert regions.
Tornadoes: Formed when warm and cold air masses collide, creating unstable rotating winds during severe thunderstorms.

Volcanoes: Eruptions destroy habitats with lava and ash, while also cooling the global climate temporarily by blocking sunlight with atmospheric particles.
Earthquakes: Shaking causes building collapses and landslides, often resulting in massive infrastructure damage and the sudden displacement of communities.
Drought: Lack of water leads to widespread crop failure, livestock loss, and severe shortages of drinking water for humans and wildlife.
Sandstorms: These storms reduce visibility to zero and cause severe respiratory issues by filling the air with fine, hazardous dust particles.
Tornadoes: Extremely high winds pulverize structures in seconds and turn debris into lethal projectiles, leaving narrow paths of total destruction.
